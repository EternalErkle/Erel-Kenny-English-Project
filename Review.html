‘The Grizzlies’ blends the classic underdog sports story with strong indigenous ideas.

Review by Erel Gililov, Richmond Hill High School
Published 9:36 PM EDT, Fri May 16, 2023
Link Copied!
The Grizzlies team's final photo before their big game in downtown Toronto.
01:13 - Source: Thegrizzliessmovie.Com
RHHS  — 

Miranda de Pencier’s The Grizzlies is a remarkable addition to Indigenous Cinema, retelling the classic underdog sports story while seamlessly illustrating the rough and brutal conditions in Kugluktuk, Nunavut. It accurately depicts how the depressed youths of Kugluktuk transformed their village, with the highest suicide rates in Canada, into a tight-knit community through the power of tradition, sport, and family. This critically acclaimed film has won eight awards and has been nominated for nine others for its amplification of indigenous ideas through an excellent depiction of a true story using acting and mise en scène.

Many of the production staff are indigenous with their views reflected closely in the film despite the director, Miranda de Pencier, being non-Indigenous. The diverse production team is a result of the considerable efforts of Indigenous films similar to The Grizzlies to accurately portray the Indigenous as capable human beings.

The film excels in displaying many unique aspects and lifestyles within the northern Indigenous community and does so very matter-of-factly. Miranda Atatahak, the inspiration for the Miranda in the film, states, “They did a really good job of bringing the Northern context to this huge movie. It does feel very much authentic.”

The positive impact of the lacrosse team on the community is not overstated in the film either. Some may be skeptical of the sudden changes that resulted from engaging in lacrosse, but many residents support the movie's depiction of real events. “ … we were, as a community, very much struggling, and then being able to live through the Grizzly experience and see all of the things that the community has built upon since then, … it's phenomenal to see,” One community member says.

Additionally, Russ Sheppard, the 23-year-old teacher in Kugluktuk, found the film very true to his memory of real events. Through lacrosse, his wish for the kids to trust in each other and himself came true. The trust built up by playing in a team together gave the students a tight bond that extended beyond the playing field and out into the Kugluktuk community.

Team bonding in 'The Grizzlies,' airing on Netflix.

There are many small details in the world building shown through mise en scène and cinematography that display the difficulty of life in northern Nunavut. In the opening scene where a young man commits suicide, the camera is angled upwards, so we can see the plane carrying Russ Sheppard flying down behind him. This early allusion to the troubles the townspeople face heavily contrasts Russ’s initially joyful appearance and shows how difficult life in Kugluktuk is.

The first notable difference between Western society and the Kugluktuk community is the terrible infrastructure. Shoddy buildings and unpaved roads line the streets while many of the youths drink alcohol on the roadside. This combined with the abuse of the youths by their parents and extremely high prices in Kugluktuk’s only convenience store gives us a strong impression of a struggling community.

When Russ arrives in Kugluktuk, we only see strained relationships between adults and youths of the community. Zach runs from his abusive father and doesn’t fight back against the residential school victim, instead choosing to live in isolation in the cold outdoors. Miranda has a strained relationship with her family and is treated like a failure despite her accomplishments and hard work.

Suicides are common in the community, with Nunavut having the highest suicide rates in Canada. Rifts between the younger and older generations stem from the mistreatment of the Indigenous in the past and present. The mise en scène reflects the emotional distance between youths and adults by placing physical distance between them unless they are in conflict. The successful introduction of lacrosse results in a fundraiser which sets the community ablaze as they raise money to send their sports team to Toronto. The enthusiastic mood of the families while fundraising heavily contrasts their debilitated appearances during the start of the film as teens and adults can be seen associating happily.

'The Grizzlies' inspires the viewer to put their all into every single day even through troubled times. It tells the viewer that one's situation can always get better if one can confide in others and try new things, just like sport and community were what brought the Kugluktuk community together. It displays numerous Indigenous views in a spin of the classic underdog story and is a worthwhile watch for all manner of viewers.

'The Grizzlies' is viewable on Netflix now. (Disclaimer: Netflix subscription is necessary.)
